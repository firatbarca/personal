Ask most people to picture climate migration and the same scene appears: long columns of people crossing a border, leaving one country for good. As another northern-hemisphere summer settles in — and with it the now-familiar run of heatwaves, failed harvests and flooded valleys — that image will be invoked again and again. It is a powerful picture. It is also, for the great majority of people on the move because of a changing climate, the wrong one.

I spend a lot of my time at the seam between climate science and public policy, and this gap between the story we tell and the evidence we have is one of the most consequential I know. Get the picture wrong and you fund the wrong responses, fear the wrong things, and overlook the people who actually need support. So it is worth being precise about how climate and human mobility really interact.

## Most movement is internal, not international

The headline finding from the serious research is consistent: when people move because of climate stress, they overwhelmingly stay inside their own country. The World Bank's *Groundswell* work suggested that, without concerted action, more than 200 million people could be moving within their own borders by 2050 across just a handful of regions. The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre, year after year, records that weather-related disasters drive far more new internal displacements than conflict does — and almost none of that shows up as a crossing into Europe or North America.

The reason is grimly practical. Crossing an international border takes money, documents, networks and a tolerance for risk that the poorest and most exposed simply do not have. Climate change tends to hit those people first and hardest. So they move the way humans have always moved under pressure: to the next town, to a relative's home, to the nearest city with work. The distance is short. Often the move is temporary — out for the dry season or the flood, back when conditions allow.

> Climate change rarely uproots people and flings them across the world. More often it nudges them down the road, and then nudges them again.

## Climate is a multiplier, not a single cause

The second thing the evidence teaches is humility about cause. There is almost never a clean line from a drought to a decision to leave. Climate acts as a *threat multiplier* — it thins out the options people already had. A family might cope with one poor harvest, but not three in a row layered on top of debt, a sick relative and a collapsing local market. The climate signal is real, but it works through economics, governance and plain bad luck.

This matters because it cuts against two opposite errors. One is denial — insisting that movement is "really" about poverty or politics, as if climate were irrelevant. The other is a kind of catastrophism that reduces complex human decisions to a single environmental trigger. Both are wrong, and both lead to bad policy. People are not passive particles swept along by rainfall data; they are making hard, deliberate choices with the options they have left.

## What this means for the people writing the cheques

If you work in ESG, development finance or corporate risk — as I do — the practical implications are unglamorous but important:

- **Adaptation beats border control.** The most humane and cost-effective interventions support people *where they are*: resilient agriculture, water security, social protection, housing in receiving towns. Money spent there reduces the desperation that turns a difficult year into a permanent departure.
- **Cities are the real frontline.** Most climate migrants end up in domestic secondary cities that are already stretched. Infrastructure, drainage and informal-settlement planning in those places is climate-mobility policy, even when no one calls it that.
- **Disclosure should ask better questions.** As frameworks mature, "climate risk" in a supply chain increasingly means labour migration, seasonal disruption and regional instability — not just stranded assets. Firms that map this honestly will be far less surprised than those tracking only physical damage to their own sites.

## Getting the picture right

None of this is a reason for complacency. Internal movement on the scale the projections describe is a profound challenge, and a portion of people genuinely will cross borders — they deserve protection and orderly routes, not a manufactured panic. But policy built on the border-crossing image tends to spend its money on walls and its political energy on fear, while the actual need sits a few hundred kilometres away, inside the country, in a city no headline will name.

The more accurate story is, in a sense, the more hopeful one. If most climate migration is internal, short and shaped by the options people have, then it is also something we can meaningfully influence — by widening those options before the choice to leave becomes the only one left. That work starts long before anyone reaches a border. It starts at home.
