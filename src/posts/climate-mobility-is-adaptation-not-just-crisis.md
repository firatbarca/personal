Every summer the images arrive on cue: cracked reservoirs, evacuations ahead of a wildfire, families on a road with whatever they could carry. This July has been no different, and the shorthand that follows is almost always the same — "climate refugees", a coming "wave", a border about to be overwhelmed. I spent my academic years studying the intersection of climate change and human mobility, and the more I read the field, the less that shorthand holds up. Most people who move because of a changing climate are not fleeing across a frontier in a single dramatic moment. They are making smaller, quieter decisions, over years, to keep a household afloat. If we want to plan honestly, we have to start by describing what is actually happening.

## The picture is mostly internal, and mostly slow

The dominant story about climate and migration is one of long-distance flight. The evidence points somewhere less cinematic. Where climate stress drives movement, it is overwhelmingly **internal** — from a drought-hit rural district to a provincial town, from a flooding delta to the edge of the nearest city — and it tends to be **gradual**, playing out through seasons and harvests rather than in a single exodus.

It is also, crucially, entangled with everything else. Almost no one moves for a single reason. A failed second harvest matters because there was already debt; a flood pushes a family out because the job that would have let them rebuild had already gone. Climate is rarely the whole cause and rarely absent — it works as a **threat multiplier**, tipping decisions that economics, conflict and opportunity had already set in motion. That makes clean attribution hard, and it makes the neat category of "climate refugee" more of a headline than a description.

> The people most exposed to climate stress are often the ones least able to move. Displacement is a symptom of vulnerability, but so is being unable to leave.

That last point deserves weight. We picture mobility as the risk, but immobility can be the greater one. Moving takes money, networks and information. The poorest households, the ones a drought hits hardest, are frequently the ones who cannot afford to relocate at all — trapped in place precisely because they have the least. Any serious policy has to hold both realities at once.

## Movement as a strategy, not only a failure

Here is the shift that changed how I read the whole subject: for a great many households, moving is not the collapse of a plan. It **is** the plan. Sending one family member to work in a city and remit money home is a centuries-old way of spreading risk across places and seasons. Seasonal and circular migration lets families ride out a bad year without abandoning land they intend to return to. When we frame all of this as crisis, we miss that much of it is competent, deliberate adaptation.

Reframing mobility as adaptation is not naïve optimism — it is a more accurate reading, and it points to better decisions. A few follow directly:

- **Keep routes open.** Legal, safe channels for seasonal and regional labour movement let people adapt without falling into smuggling networks or dangerous crossings.
- **Invest where people arrive, not only where they leave.** The secondary towns and city fringes absorbing internal movement need housing, water and services planned in advance, not retrofitted after arrival.
- **Support the people who stay.** Remittances, local adaptation and social protection help households remain in place by choice rather than by entrapment.
- **Plan relocation with communities, not at them.** Where land genuinely becomes unliveable, planned relocation works only when the people affected help design it — otherwise it fails slowly and expensively.

None of this is soft. It is cheaper and steadier than managing repeated emergencies, and it treats people as agents making rational choices rather than as a threat to be repelled.

## Why this belongs on the sustainability desk

I write mostly about ESG and reporting, so let me connect the two worlds I live in. Human mobility is quietly becoming a material issue for organisations, not only a humanitarian one. Supply chains lean on agricultural regions and labour markets that climate stress is reshaping. Workforces move. The **social** and **governance** pillars of any serious sustainability assessment now have to reckon with how a changing climate moves people — and how a company's own operations, water use and land footprint affect the communities deciding whether to stay or go.

That is the same discipline I return to again and again: look past the headline number to the conditions underneath it. A drought statistic, like an emissions figure, only means something when you can trace it back to the lives and decisions it describes.

## The point

Climate mobility is not a wave breaking on a border. It is a slow tide of ordinary people rearranging their lives to stay afloat — mostly close to home, mostly for more than one reason, and often with real skill. If we keep calling it a crisis, we will keep building walls against the wrong thing. If we call it what it largely is — adaptation — we can start doing the unglamorous, effective work of helping it go well. The heat is not going to relent. The least we can do is describe honestly the people living in it.
