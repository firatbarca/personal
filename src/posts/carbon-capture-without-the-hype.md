Few climate technologies split a room as fast as **Carbon Capture, Utilisation and Storage (CCUS)**. To its champions it's the bridge that lets heavy industry keep running while the world decarbonises. To its critics it's a licence to pollute, dressed up in engineering. After years of watching decarbonisation plans meet the hard edge of a balance sheet, I've landed somewhere less dramatic than either camp: CCUS is neither saviour nor scam. It's a specialised tool — valuable for a narrow set of jobs, and actively harmful when used as an excuse to avoid the easier wins.

The question worth asking isn't "is CCUS good or bad?" It's "where does it genuinely earn its place — and where is it a distraction?"

## What CCUS actually is

Strip away the acronym and CCUS is three linked steps.

**Capture** separates CO₂ from an industrial gas stream — the flue gas of a cement kiln, a steel furnace, a power plant — before it reaches the atmosphere. (A related family, *direct air capture*, pulls CO₂ straight from ambient air, though at far higher cost and energy.)

**Utilisation** puts the captured CO₂ to work — as a feedstock for chemicals, synthetic fuels, or building materials, or, more controversially, to force additional oil out of ageing wells.

**Storage** injects the CO₂ deep underground into geological formations — depleted oil and gas reservoirs, saline aquifers — where it is meant to stay for centuries.

The gap between the "U" and the "S" matters far more than the tidy acronym suggests, and I'll come back to it.

## Where it helps — and where it doesn't

CCUS reduces emissions in the obvious way: CO₂ that would have gone up a chimney goes underground instead. But its real value is narrower than that sentence implies.

For much of the economy — electricity, cars, buildings — we already have cleaner and increasingly cost-competitive answers: renewables, electrification, efficiency. Spending scarce capital to capture emissions from a coal plant that could instead be replaced by wind and solar is, in most cases, the wrong trade.

Where CCUS becomes genuinely important is the set of **hard-to-abate** sectors — the ones with no easy electric substitute:

- **Cement**, where much of the CO₂ comes from the chemistry of making clinker — calcination releases CO₂ inherently — not only from burning fuel.
- **Steel**, **chemicals**, and **fertilisers**, where intense heat and process reactions are difficult to electrify.
- Some **waste-to-energy** and heavy industrial-heat applications.

In these places, capture isn't avoiding an easier fix — it's one of the only fixes on offer. That is the honest case for CCUS: not everywhere, but for the emissions we don't yet know how to eliminate.

> CCUS earns its keep on the emissions we can't yet avoid — and wastes money on the ones we can.

## The honest ledger

The benefits are real: a route to cut emissions from industries that underpin modern infrastructure, the potential to build a new value chain and the skilled jobs that come with it, and — for permanent geological storage — one of the few ways to lock carbon away at industrial scale.

The challenges are just as real, and worth naming plainly:

- **Cost and the energy penalty.** Capturing CO₂ takes energy, which raises the cost of whatever you're making. Without a carbon price or subsidy, it often does not pay for itself.
- **Utilisation isn't always removal.** Several common uses — synthetic fuels and carbonated drinks among them — release the CO₂ again when the product is burned or consumed. Only durable storage, or genuinely permanent products, delivers a lasting climate benefit. A lot of "carbon utilisation" marketing quietly skips past this.
- **Infrastructure.** Pipelines and storage sites don't exist at scale in most regions, and building them is slow and capital-heavy.
- **Verification and trust.** You have to prove the CO₂ stays where you put it — robust measurement, reporting, and verification — and win public confidence about storage beneath communities' feet.
- **Moral hazard.** The biggest risk isn't technical. It's that the promise of future capture becomes a reason to delay cuts that are cheaper and available today.

## How a business should actually use it

For any company, CCUS belongs near the **bottom** of the decarbonisation order, not the top. The sequence that survives scrutiny is familiar:

1. **Avoid and reduce first** — efficiency, process change, materials.
2. **Switch to clean energy** — electrify, and generate or buy renewables wherever you can.
3. **Then capture the residual** — use CCUS for the emissions that are genuinely left over, the ones with no current alternative.

Used in that order, CCUS is a credible part of a net-zero plan. Used as step one — "we'll keep emitting and capture it later" — it's greenwashing with a compressor attached, and increasingly a disclosure and reputational risk under frameworks like the **CSRD**, which expect a real transition plan rather than a hypothetical one.

It also helps to stop framing CCUS as a rival to other strategies. Renewables decarbonise energy; efficiency cuts demand; offsets, at best, handle emissions elsewhere; CCUS addresses the specific industrial CO₂ the others can't reach. They aren't competitors — they're a toolkit, and good plans use each for what it does best.

## The point

CCUS will matter for climate mitigation — but only if we're honest about its scope. Its adoption improves when three things line up: a **carbon price or incentive** that makes capture pay, **shared infrastructure** — pipelines and storage — that no single company can justify alone, and **rigorous verification** so that a captured tonne is a trusted tonne. Policy and finance will do more to unlock CCUS than any single technical breakthrough.

For businesses, the discipline is easy to state and harder to hold: cut what you can, capture only what you genuinely can't, and never let the promise of capture become an excuse to keep emitting. Treated as a scalpel rather than a shield, CCUS is one of the more valuable tools we have for the hardest part of the climate problem. Treated as a licence to carry on as before, it's just an expensive way to stand still.
