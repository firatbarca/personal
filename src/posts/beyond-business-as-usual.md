For decades, **Business as Usual (BAU)** has been the default way to run an organisation. It rests on a quiet assumption: that today's business models, operations, supply chains, and investment decisions can carry on with only incremental improvement — that tomorrow will look more or less like yesterday. In much of the corporate world, BAU simply means keeping things running the way they have always run, without rethinking how value is created or how environmental impact is managed.

That assumption held up while climate change was treated as an environmental footnote. It doesn't anymore. Having spent my working life inside reporting and ESG cycles, I've watched climate move from the sustainability team's corner into the centre of the business — risk, regulation, finance, operations, and long-term competitiveness. BAU is now one of the riskiest positions an organisation can hold, precisely because it still looks like the safe one.

## Understanding Business as Usual

In climate science and economics, Business as Usual is a **baseline scenario** — a reference point for what happens if current policies, technologies, and behaviours simply continue without meaningful intervention.

Inside an organisation the idea is much the same: keep operating the established way, make investment decisions on traditional criteria, and replace technology or infrastructure only when it reaches the end of its normal life.

The problem is inheritance. Most of these systems were designed in an era when greenhouse gas emissions and resource consumption were never part of a strategic decision. The machinery still runs on those old assumptions.

## Why Business as Usual matters for climate change

Anthropogenic climate change is driven by greenhouse gases accumulating in the atmosphere — largely from burning fossil fuels, industrial production, agriculture, and land-use change.

Most BAU practice still leans on exactly those systems:

- Dependence on fossil-fuel-based energy.
- Carbon-intensive manufacturing.
- Supply chains designed without regard for climate impact.
- Little integration of climate risk into investment and operating decisions.
- Incremental tweaks in place of transformational change.

Multiply that across industries and economies, and emissions stay stubbornly higher than any lower-carbon alternative would produce. Business as Usual isn't neutral — it actively holds the line at the high-emissions setting.

## The hidden cost of the status quo

BAU is not only about the emissions of today. It also sets how quickly an organisation can adapt to a changing world.

Factories, buildings, fleets, energy infrastructure — most industrial assets are built to run for decades. Keep investing in or leaning on high-emission assets and you extend their footprint far into the future. This is **carbon lock-in**: a convenient choice today quietly mortgaging tomorrow.

> Business as Usual is, at its core, a bet that tomorrow will look like yesterday. On climate, that's the one bet the evidence no longer supports.

Delay carries its own price. As governments tighten climate policy, markets shift, and clean technologies mature, the organisations that put off adaptation tend to face a harder, costlier transition later. And all the while they carry growing exposure to the physical side of climate change — extreme heat, flooding, drought, supply-chain disruption — risks that increasingly belong in enterprise risk management, not the sustainability appendix.

## Why Business as Usual no longer fits modern ESG

**Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG)** frameworks ask organisations to understand and manage their environmental and social impact while strengthening governance and transparency. That is a real departure from BAU.

Instead of optimising only for short-term operational or financial performance, ESG pushes harder questions:

- What are our greenhouse gas emissions?
- Where do our greatest climate-related risks sit?
- How resilient is our supply chain?
- Can we use resources more efficiently?
- Are sustainability considerations actually built into strategic decisions?

None of these can be answered by carrying on as before. They demand measuring impact, naming risks, setting targets, and wiring sustainability into the decisions that matter.

## From compliance to strategy

This is changing what sustainability *is* inside a company.

It used to sit to one side of the core business, driven mainly by regulatory compliance. Increasingly it runs through corporate strategy, investment planning, procurement, product development, and risk management.

Behind that shift is a simple recognition: climate-related risk has operational, financial, and strategic consequences, and resilience now depends on how well an organisation can anticipate and respond to them.

## Looking ahead

Business as Usual underwrote decades of economic growth. But the conditions it assumed are dissolving. Climate change, shifting stakeholder expectations, fast-moving technology, and the transition to a lower-carbon economy are all redrawing the landscape a business has to operate in.

Organisations that keep relying solely on the old operating model will find it harder to manage emerging risk, meet tightening reporting expectations, and stay competitive.

Moving beyond Business as Usual, then, isn't only a matter of environmental responsibility. It's about readiness — building the kind of organisation that treats sustainability, resilience, and long-term value creation not as add-ons, but as the foundations of a strategy that survives contact with the future.
