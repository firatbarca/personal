Every heatwave brings the same headline back: a coming wave of climate refugees, millions on the move, borders under strain. It is a vivid image, and this summer — with much of the Northern Hemisphere baking again — it is everywhere. My background sits at the meeting point of climate policy and human mobility, and I want to push gently against that picture. Not because displacement is not real or serious. It is. But because the geography in the headline is usually wrong, and getting the geography wrong leads to spending money and political attention in the wrong places.

The evidence is fairly settled on one point: most people displaced by climate stress do not cross an international border. They move within their own country, often over short distances, and frequently they intend to return. If our mental model is a transcontinental exodus, we will build the wrong institutions to meet a mostly domestic reality.

## The picture the data actually paints

Start with the numbers we do have. Year after year, weather-related events — floods, storms, drought — displace far more people inside their own borders than any conflict does. The overwhelming majority of that movement is internal. Someone whose home floods moves to the next town, to a relative, to a temporary shelter; they do not, in the main, set off for another continent.

That matters for three practical reasons.

- **Distance.** Crossing a border takes money, documents, and networks. The people most exposed to climate shocks are often the ones with the least of all three, so they move the shortest distance they can manage.
- **Intent.** A lot of climate-linked movement is temporary and circular — leave during the dry season or after a flood, return when conditions allow. Treating every departure as permanent resettlement misreads what people are actually doing.
- **Slow onset.** The dramatic flood makes the news, but the bigger driver is slow-onset change — declining yields, saltwater creeping into farmland, water tables falling. That erodes livelihoods gradually, and people respond by moving in stages, not in a single dramatic wave.

> The question that matters is rarely "will they come here?" It is "what happens in the place they leave, and the place ten miles down the road where they arrive?"

## Why the "refugee wave" framing costs us

The wave framing is not just inaccurate; it is expensive. When climate mobility is filed under border security, budgets and attention flow to the frontier — to fences, patrols, and processing. Meanwhile the actual pressure lands on domestic systems that rarely get a mention: the secondary town whose water and housing were not built for a fifth more residents, the region losing its working-age farmers, the informal settlement that swells after each bad season.

There is also a legal trap in the word *refugee*. The 1951 Refugee Convention protects people fleeing persecution; it was not written for someone whose land stopped producing. Very few climate-displaced people meet its definition, so leaning on "climate refugee" as a category promises a legal shelter that, for most, simply is not there. It raises expectations we cannot meet and distracts from the protections we could actually build — planned relocation, adaptation finance, and mobility that is safe and orderly rather than desperate.

None of this is an argument for complacency about cross-border movement. Some regions — low-lying island states above all — face genuinely existential questions where a border may have to be crossed. Those cases deserve serious, tailored responses. But they are the exception that proves the rule, not the template for the whole phenomenon.

## What better policy looks like

If most movement is internal and gradual, the useful levers are mostly domestic and unglamorous.

- **Invest where people arrive.** Secondary cities and receiving regions need housing, water, schools, and jobs planned in advance — not improvised after each shock.
- **Keep return an option.** Support in the places people leave — climate-resilient agriculture, restored water systems — lets circular migration stay circular, so a bad season does not become a one-way exit.
- **Make moving a choice, not a last resort.** Where land is genuinely becoming unliveable, planned, consultative relocation beats waiting for a disaster to force the decision.
- **Measure honestly.** For the growing number of organisations reporting on climate risk, "communities affected by displacement" is becoming a real disclosure line. Count internal and temporary movement, not just the border crossings that make headlines.

That last point is where this connects to the reporting work I usually write about. As climate risk moves into non-financial disclosure, human mobility will increasingly show up in the data — in supply chains that depend on a stable rural workforce, in operations sited near retreating coastlines. Companies and governments that understand the real shape of climate mobility will plan for it soberly. Those still watching the border for a wave will keep being surprised by what was, all along, happening much closer to home.
