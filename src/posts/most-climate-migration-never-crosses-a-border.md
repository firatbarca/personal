The stock image of climate migration — a crowded boat, a fenced border, a queue of "climate refugees" waiting to be let in somewhere richer — is the one that travels furthest in public debate and explains the least about what is actually happening. I spend most of my working life in the world of ESG and sustainability data, but my training is in political science, and human mobility is the question that first pulled me into this field. As I write this in the middle of another European heatwave, with wildfires and flash floods competing for the same headlines, it feels worth saying plainly: the way we picture climate migration is mostly wrong, and getting the picture right changes what we should do about it.

## The picture in our heads is the wrong one

When people say "climate migration," they almost always mean someone leaving a poor country for a wealthy one. The reality is far more domestic. The overwhelming majority of climate-related movement happens **inside a person's own country**, often over short distances, and frequently on a temporary or seasonal basis.

The numbers bear this out. The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre counts tens of millions of new internal displacements every year, the clear majority of them triggered by weather-related hazards — storms, floods, droughts — rather than conflict. And when the World Bank modelled longer-term trends in its Groundswell work, it projected that, on current trajectories, as many as 216 million people could move *within* their own countries by 2050. Not across the Mediterranean. Across a province.

This matters because the border image quietly imports a whole set of assumptions: that climate migration is somebody else's crisis, that it is best managed at the frontier, and that the right instruments are visas and patrol boats. Reframe it as an internal phenomenon and a different toolkit comes into view — urban planning, regional investment, social protection.

## Slow onset moves people differently than a flood

It also helps to separate two very different kinds of movement. A flood or a cyclone produces **sudden displacement**: people leave in a hurry, often return once the water recedes, and are counted, if at all, as a spike. Slow-onset change — drought, soil salinisation, creeping sea-level rise, a growing season that no longer holds — works differently. It rarely produces a dramatic exodus. It erodes a livelihood until a household sends one member to a nearby town for wage work, then another, until the centre of gravity has quietly shifted without anyone ever declaring themselves a migrant.

Much of this looks less like flight and more like adaptation: circular labour migration, a move to the nearest secondary city, remittances flowing back to keep a rural home viable. It is easy to miss precisely because it is undramatic.

And then there is the hardest part of the story, the one the refugee image erases entirely.

> The cruel irony of climate mobility is that those with the least to fall back on are often the ones who cannot leave at all.

Moving costs money, contacts, and information. Researchers call the households that would move but cannot **trapped populations** — and they are typically the poorest and most exposed. A framing obsessed with arrivals at a border tends to ignore them completely, because their tragedy is that they never arrive anywhere.

## The legal category that isn't

Part of the confusion is baked into our language. There is, strictly speaking, no such thing as a "climate refugee" in law. The 1951 Refugee Convention protects people fleeing persecution on specific grounds; it was never written with a changing climate in mind, and someone displaced by a failing harvest fits none of its categories. The phrase has real rhetorical power — it conveys urgency and injustice — but it points to a status that does not exist, which leaves the people it describes in a governance gap: too displaced to be called voluntary migrants, too outside the Convention to be called refugees.

## What follows if we get the frame right

None of this is an argument for complacency. It is an argument for aiming our attention at the places movement actually happens. A few practical shifts follow directly:

- **Plan for the receiving towns, not just the borders.** Most people move to the nearest city they can reach; the secondary towns absorbing them need water, housing, and jobs long before any frontier does.
- **Treat mobility as adaptation, not only as failure.** For many households, the ability to move is a coping strategy worth supporting — not a breakdown to be prevented.
- **Invest so that staying stays possible.** The dignified option is often to help people remain where they are, which makes local climate adaptation a mobility policy too.
- **Count it honestly.** As in any reporting discipline, decisions follow the data we bother to collect — and internal, gradual movement is exactly the kind that goes uncounted.

Climate change will move people this century; that much is settled. The open question is whether we meet it with the caricature at the border or with the quieter, more accurate story of people making hard choices close to home. I know which one leads to better policy — and, I think, to more humane ones.
